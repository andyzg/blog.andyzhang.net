"The prime factors of 13195 are 5, 7, 13 and 29.

What is the largest prime factor of the number 600851475143 ?"

I had a lot of fun going through this one because I realized the problems slowly got harder and harder. To start, here's my solution:

[code language="python"]

print(filter(lambda x:not sum([x%i==0 for i in range(2,x)]),filter(lambda x:x!=0,[x if 600851475143%x == 0 else 0 for x in range(2, int( 600851475143**0.5))]))[-1])

[/code]

I start off by making a list of all the factors between 2 and the square root of 600851475143. This gives me a list of a LOT of zeros and a couple of factors which I then filter out based on whether they're equal to 0 or not.

[code language="python"]

filter(lambda x:x!=0,[x if 600851475143%x == 0 else 0 for x in range(2, int( 600851475143**0.5))])

[/code]

After this, I needed to find the largest prime factor. Since not all of them were prime, I needed to find a way to figure them out. I opted for the simple way where I iterated through the numbers 2 to x-1 and checked whether or not they were divisible or not.

In the case where the number was prime, all values would have returned false, meaning the sum would give 0 since false = 0. However, if there was a factor, this would mean that there would've existed at least 1 element that was 1 inside the list. By summing the list, I could see whether or not a factor existed or not and I simply complemented the value in order to obtain the boolean determining whether the number was prime or not.

Since this list was already sorted, I would just print out the last element of the list by appending [-1] to the end:
